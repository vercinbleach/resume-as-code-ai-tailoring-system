# Technical bullet patterns

Use only the section matching the target role. Replace bracketed fields with
supported evidence; never treat placeholders as suggested facts.

## Software engineering

Useful evidence:

- Performance: latency, throughput, runtime, query time, response time.
- Reliability: uptime, failure rate, incidents, backlog, recovery time.
- Scale: users, requests, services, workflows, data volume, integrations.
- Efficiency: engineering hours, manual steps, infrastructure or vendor cost.
- Delivery: release frequency, migration scope, lead time, adoption.
- Security: permissions, attack surface, vulnerabilities, auditability.
- Quality: tests, coverage, regressions, defect rate, CI status.

Patterns:

- `[Improved outcome] by [verified change] by [technical action].`
- `Built [system] using [material technologies], handling [verified scale] and enabling [result].`
- `Reduced [failure or latency] from [before] to [after] by [architecture or implementation].`
- `Automated [workflow], eliminating [verified manual work or delay] through [method].`
- `Designed [component] under [constraint], enabling [supported capability].`
- `Integrated [supported systems], centralizing [data, approvals, documents, or process state] and enabling [verified operational result].`

Accurate verbs include built, implemented, designed, integrated, automated,
migrated, optimized, hardened, instrumented, debugged, and maintained. Use
architected only when system-level design ownership is supported.

## Data and AI

Useful evidence:

- Data volume, sources, update frequency, processing duration, freshness.
- Data quality, validation rate, error reduction, completeness.
- Model or evaluator quality with a named method and supported test set.
- Cost, latency, reliability, failure detection, regression coverage.
- Decision or product outcome enabled by analysis or automation.
- Human review, monitoring, safety gates, and operational adoption.

Patterns:

- `Built [pipeline or system] that [processed or evaluated scope], producing [supported outcome].`
- `Improved [quality, cost, or latency] by [verified amount] through [data or model method].`
- `Implemented [evaluation or validation method] across [scope], detecting [supported failure class].`
- `Analyzed [dataset or process] to identify [finding], enabling [decision or result].`
- `Operationalized [AI capability] with [monitoring, review, or safety controls], supporting [use case].`

Accurate verbs include analyzed, modeled, evaluated, validated, normalized,
instrumented, monitored, processed, visualized, and operationalized. Do not
claim model accuracy, autonomous behavior, production use, or causality unless
the evidence establishes it.

## Engineering management and technical leadership

Useful evidence:

- Direct reports, team size, disciplines, or cross-functional participants.
- Delivery outcome achieved through the team.
- Prioritization, planning, trade-offs, and decision ownership.
- Mentoring, onboarding, feedback, delegation, and growth.
- Stakeholder alignment, dependency management, and conflict resolution.
- Reliability, delivery, product, or organizational improvement.

Patterns:

- `Led [supported team scope] to deliver [outcome] by [leadership action].`
- `Coordinated [teams or stakeholders] across [scope], enabling [result].`
- `Introduced [planning or delivery mechanism], improving [supported outcome].`
- `Mentored [supported scope] through [specific action], resulting in [supported development outcome].`
- `Made [technical or product decision] under [constraint], balancing [trade-off] and achieving [result].`
- `Partnered directly with [supported executive or Board scope] to [prioritize, align, or decide], enabling [verified delivery or organizational result].`

Use managed only for documented direct people management. Use led only when
decision, direction, coordination, or outcome ownership is established. A team
result must not be presented as an individual implementation result.

## High-value clarification questions

Ask the smallest relevant subset:

- What did you personally design, implement, decide, or lead?
- What changed after your work?
- Is there a verified before-and-after measure?
- What was the production, user, data, workflow, or team scale?
- Which constraints or trade-offs made the work difficult?
- Was the result deployed, adopted, tested, or only demonstrated as a PoC?
- Who benefited, and how was that observed?
- For management, were these direct reports or cross-functional collaborators?
- Was the executive or Board relationship a formal reporting line, recurring
  direct collaboration, or a presentation audience, and what decision or
  delivery outcome resulted?

## Reject these transformations

- Adding a number because every bullet is expected to have one.
- Estimating percentages, money, time, users, or team size.
- Replacing a missing outcome with activity volume that has no relevance.
- Listing technologies without explaining their role.
- Turning collaboration into leadership or implementation into architecture.
- Moving or combining a claim across employers, roles, or projects.
- Reusing one accomplishment or metric as if it supported multiple achievements.
- Adding soft skills, assumed or basic skills, outdated technologies, or
  unsupported proficiency levels.
- Claiming production, adoption, accuracy, reliability, or savings from sequence
  or plausibility alone.
- Repeating job-description keywords that the evidence does not support.
- Using executive or Board proximity as prestige without a supported action,
  decision, or outcome.
- Combining coaching and executive prioritization in one bullet when they are
  separate achievements; place each relationship with the outcome it shaped.
- Adding a summary, objective, headline, or section, or changing contact,
  Education, chronology, experience metadata, or Jake order in pipeline mode.
